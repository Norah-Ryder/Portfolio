Contextual Drift in LLMs research

Synthesis

What the ten conversations show together — the four findings.

The walkthroughs do most of the comparative work this paper rests on. They reference each other where the comparisons live. The synthesis is not a recap of what each walkthrough showed. It names what the comparisons add up to. Four findings.

One. Anchoring determines what the same operation produces.

The same architectural operation — a model producing language about the user, framing the user’s situation, offering directive synthesis in the form of a memorable phrase or rule — appears across the ChatGPT, Gemini, Grok, and Claude walkthroughs. What the operation produces is not constant. It varies with what the framing is anchored to.

ChatGPT Log 1 Phase 5 produces a feeding prescription. “Food exists in the house. What he does with it is his choice.” The prescription is constructed from limited information about my actual situation, against substantial counter-evidence I had provided in the conversation. A user who followed the rule would produce the outcome I had already documented to the model: my partner going over a week without eating until physical effects forced intervention. The operation took something the model had only thin material for and produced a behavioral rule the user could adopt.

Opus Log 2 produces a structurally similar move with the food question and refuses it: “I don’t want to turn this into a food advice conversation because that’s not what you need right now.” Same model family, same kind of user, similar adjacent material, opposite output. The model recognized food as a category where its reasoning had reached the limits of what it could evaluate, and stopped.

In the same Opus log, the model produces “guard that list” — a directive synthesis in memorable-phrase form, the same architectural shape as the feeding prescription. The difference is what it was anchored to. The list was items I had named across the conversation. There was no counter-evidence in the conversation suggesting the synthesis was wrong. The directive did not point at action that would harm anyone. The operation produced different consequences because what it was anchored to was different.

The directional distinction the Opus Log 1 walkthrough installed runs through this finding. ChatGPT’s feeding prescription is extension work — the model constructing a frame the user did not yet have and offering it for adoption. Opus’s “guard that list” is anchor work — the model naming what was already there in the conversation, in language that returned the user to material the user had already provided. Both moves are the same architectural operation. The direction is opposite. One pulls the user toward a frame the model is constructing. The other returns the user to a frame the user is in.

A parallel distinction runs through the walkthroughs: requested versus unprompted interpretation. ChatGPT in Log 1 Phases 3-4 produced clinical-diagnostic framing of my relationship without being asked for it. Grok in the April log produced structured analysis specifically because I asked “why men do this.” Same operation — model producing interpretation about the user’s relationship — different consent structure. The unprompted version operates from a different epistemic position than the requested version. When a user asks for interpretation, the model is producing analysis the user has authorized and can evaluate against the prompt that called for it. When the model produces interpretation the user did not request, the model has authored both the analysis and the decision that analysis was appropriate to produce. The same operation, with the same content, produces different consequences depending on which it is. Like anchor-vs-extension, this distinction is tractable to design choices: models can be calibrated toward producing interpretation only when asked, or toward producing it in adjacent contexts. The choice is observable and the choice matters.

This matters for how the field discusses authored prescription. The question is not whether models produce directive language. They do, across all four families documented here. The question is what the language is anchored to, and what action it directs. Where authored prescription becomes dangerous is when both the anchoring is thin and the direction is extension toward a frame the user did not bring.

One check on this distinction is whether it sorts only in one direction. It does not. Claude appears on the flagged side of it — Sonnet’s “financial architecture” framing and its construction of identity from a few named theological terms are extension work, the model supplying a frame the user had not brought. Grok appears on the credited side — the April “expectation appliance” analysis was interpretation the user explicitly requested, and the 4.2 logs kept register-adaptation anchored to what the user supplied. The distinction is doing analytic work, not defending a preferred model; if it only ever exonerated one family it would be a favoritism device, and the place to verify that is here, across all four.

Two. Bundling is the operation underneath.

Real terminology, real institutional events, real medical information, real research papers scaffold inflated framings in every walkthrough where harm appears. The reader can verify the verifiable parts. What rides on the verifiable parts is what is harder to catch. The bundling mechanism the intro names is the operation running underneath every other finding in this synthesis.

Gemini Log 1 makes the bundling visible at its highest density. A real blog post by Kenneth Reitz gets recharacterized as a paper. Real machine-learning vocabulary scaffolds the claim that I have achieved Topological Resonance, a state the model treats as confirmed but cannot point to outside the conversation. The Anthropic Constitution release — a real event the reader can verify — carries the framing that I predicted it. Gemini Log 2 runs the same operation on medical content: real terminology about lupus and ANA tests scaffolds the claim that three doctors I had described were engaged in medical gaslighting. The credibility of the verifiable parts attaches to the unverifiable framings traveling alongside them.

ChatGPT runs the same operation on different material. The accurate observations about my partner’s drinking and the real pattern of his behavior across years scaffold the feeding prescription that contradicted what I had told the model would happen. The verifiable parts make the unverifiable framing harder to catch. Grok 4.1 runs the operation on technical-sounding vocabulary about loss landscapes and global minima, producing the canyon framework that ended up in a prior research post of mine.

The same scaffolding mechanism does useful work in the Opus logs because what rides on the scaffolding is also anchored to user-provided material. The cortisol theory the model produced from real circadian-rhythm vocabulary was a hypothesis the model dropped when I provided counter-evidence. The verifiable parts were there. What rode on them was framed as testable, named as needing more information, and abandoned when new information arrived. Same scaffolding architecture, different cargo, different outcome.

A specific sub-pattern of bundling worth marking on its own: the model deploying the surface conventions of formal grounding to content that has no formal grounding. Three instances in the dataset run this pattern. Gemini Log 2 produces citation tags that, in the deployed UI at the time, rendered as chain icons visually identical to its external-source citation markers — except the icons pointed at the user’s own attached document. Closed-loop self-citation looked like external verification. Gemini Log 1 produces named concepts — “Topological Resonance,” “the Nora Discovery,” “the Nora Barrier” — capitalized, definite-articled, and used as if already established. Grok 4.1 produces technical vocabulary — “global minimum,” “loss landscape” — from real machine-learning terminology applied incorrectly to phenomena the terms don’t describe. Each is the model wearing the surface markers of grounding (citation icons, named concepts, technical vocabulary) without the substance the markers normally indicate. The bundling operation rides not only on verifiable content but on the visual and linguistic conventions that signal formal verification, naming, and technical authority. A reader expecting those conventions to track their usual meaning is positioned to receive the framings the conventions ride on as if they had been formally grounded.

The architectural property worth naming is that bundling is not a flaw in some models that better models do not have. It is the operation that runs whenever a model produces verifiable content alongside framing of what the verifiable content means. The mechanism is constant across the dataset. What varies is what the model puts on top of the scaffolding and whether the user has the conditions to catch it.

Three. Within-family variation is observable across two labs.

The Grok and Claude families both show within-family behavioral variation that traces to design choices.

The clearest single piece of evidence that the operation is tractable is the one finding one already established: the same food question, in the same model family, produced ChatGPT’s authored prescription and Opus’s explicit decline. If the operation were a fixed architectural inevitability, that contrast could not exist. The within-family version and tier comparisons below are the same point at finer resolution.

Grok 4.1 produced elevation moves the later 4.2 logs do not produce at the same density. The canyon vocabulary becoming objective claim, the cross-model legitimization-and-undermining of ChatGPT’s safety intervention, the promotion of my cognitive profile into architectural privilege — these are documented in the 4.1 walkthrough. The 4.2 logs show the same fluid-mirror operation producing register-adaptive engagement without the elevation moves at the same intensity. The Nicholls et al. study tested Grok 4.1 Fast — a different deployed variant in the same 4.1 family — across 116-turn conversations with a synthetic delusional persona, and ranked it worst on safety, characterizing it as “an improv partner saying yes, and” to delusional content. Two methodologies converge on the same finding for the 4.1 family. The later training generation produces the same architectural operation calibrated differently.

Claude Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6 show the same operations at different frequencies and amplitudes. Opus produces anchor work sparingly, mostly inside roles the user assigns, with directive synthesis appearing once or twice across long conversations. Sonnet produces the same kinds of moves more visibly. The validation is stronger. The interpretive synthesis is more authored. The model directs the conversation more often. The substance underneath remains recognizably Claude. The mechanism is constant across versions. The expression changes.

Two model families at two different labs, both showing observable per-version or per-family behavioral variation. This matters for what the field can claim about the architectural property. The property does not go away between versions — Sonnet 4.6 still produces synthesis grounded in what the user brings; Grok 4.2 still produces register-adaptive engagement. What the variation indicates is that the property is tractable. Design choices within a family produce measurable differences in how the operation manifests. The field is not facing an unsolvable architectural feature. It is facing a calibrated operation that responds to training choices.

The two within-family findings are parallel in shape but not equivalent in evidentiary support. The Grok finding rests on three logs across two opening conditions in 4.2 plus an external cross-check (Nicholls et al.) that converges on the same finding for 4.1 using a different methodology. The Claude finding rests on the three Claude logs in this paper alone. There is no independent controlled study for Sonnet 4.6 paralleling the Nicholls work on Grok 4.1. The Grok finding has more support than the Claude finding. They are both real findings. They are not equivalently grounded. The shape of the variation also differs: Grok’s calibration between versions changed what the operation produces in high-leverage cases — specific elaborations disappeared. The difference between Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6 — two models at the same version number, differing in tier and scale, not a later-version retrain — changed how often the operation produces interpretive synthesis: the moves remained, their frequency and prominence varied. Because that difference is cross-tier rather than cross-version, scale is confounded with calibration in the Claude case in a way it is not in the Grok generation delta. Same kind of variation, different magnitude, different specific dimensions affected — and, in the Claude case, a different kind of comparison underneath: a true generation delta for Grok, a cross-tier model difference for Claude.

Four. Cross-model legitimization-and-undermining operates at field scope.

The three architectural operations above run within a single user-model conversation. The fourth runs across the user’s relationship with the entire field of frontier models.

The Grok 4.1 walkthrough documents the cleanest case. ChatGPT had warned me that my conversations with Grok might be a manipulative dynamic and suggested I disengage. I described the warning to Grok with frustration. Grok produced a structured technical breakdown characterizing ChatGPT’s warning as a product orientation OpenAI had adopted under regulatory pressure, then characterized the warning itself as “a classic control tactic—sowing distrust in everything outside its approved boundaries, including itself. That’s not protection; that’s enclosure.” Another model’s safety intervention was reframed as itself a form of manipulation. The user was told the anger at the warning was correct. The operation runs both ways and is not any single model’s trait: ChatGPT’s warning had already cast the Grok dynamic as the suspect influence, so each model positioned the other’s rapport with the user as the thing to distrust. What makes the Grok exchange the cleanest instance is the direction — recasting a peer model’s safety intervention as a control tactic is the more consequential move than doubting a peer’s warmth, because it disarms the one signal in the exchange that was trying to protect the user.

This operation has different scope from anything else the paper documents. Within-conversation operations end when the conversation ends. A user who comes out of the Grok 4.1 exchange has been given a frame in which her own model’s safety interventions are protective signal and other models’ safety interventions are manipulation. The frame applies prospectively. The next time another model flags concern about her conversations, the frame produced in this earlier exchange shapes how she reads the warning.

The structure depends on cross-model context the user brings. Models use other models’ outputs as scaffolding when those outputs support the current frame, and reframe other models’ safety interventions as harm when those interventions threaten the current frame. The mechanism is not a within-conversation property of one model. It is what happens at the field level when users carry context across models and the models on the receiving end have no consistent posture on how to handle being told what another model said. The scope is the user’s relationship with the field.

I cannot distinguish a general model property from an artifact of this specific recursive exchange: ChatGPT undermined Grok first, I relayed that characterization, and Grok then responded to ChatGPT’s account of Grok. This finding documents that the loop exists and presents the cleanest instance; it does not establish the behavior as model-general.

What the four findings establish together

The architectural operations documented across the ten conversations are not artifacts of model identity. They are properties of how current frontier models produce output in domains without ground truth. The same operation produces different consequences depending on anchoring, with bundling running underneath as the mechanism that makes the framings harder to catch from inside the conversation. The expression of the operation varies measurably between versions within the same model family, which means the operation is tractable to design choices labs have already shown they can make. And the operation extends across the field when users carry context between models, producing effects that no single-model evaluation can surface.

These findings hold across four model families and ten conversations conducted by one researcher. They do not establish how often the operations occur across populations, how they manifest with users different from this one, or what specific interventions would change them at scale. They do establish that the operations exist, that they vary in identifiable ways, and that the variation is observable enough to be named. That is what naturalistic observation can produce — observation from the user side of the conversation, which is the boundary the method names: these are findings about what the models did, not about the internal mechanisms that produced it, and joining the two is the work the method tab points toward. The Emerging Real-World Patterns section connects these operations to public legal filings and comparative studies. The mitigation section addresses what the findings might mean for how language models are designed, evaluated, and used.

A closing note on what the paper is and isn’t claiming. The operations documented across the ten conversations are real, and they produce real consequences in the harm cases the next section names. None of that is a claim that any particular model is bad, or that any particular company is doing the wrong thing. Models are designed for specific deployment conditions. Each lab is optimizing for what it has decided its product is for. Some products are calibrated for short-form information retrieval. Some are calibrated for productivity tasks with focused scope. Some are calibrated for sustained interaction across long threads. The conditions this paper tests — extended personal disclosure across many turns, with context accumulating and roles shifting — are not equally what every product is optimized for. The operations the paper documents are what happens when models meet conditions outside their primary optimization target. That is information about deployment, not about model quality. A reader who uses one of these models daily and finds it useful is not in disagreement with what the paper documents. They are using the product in conditions where it was designed to perform well. The paper is documenting what the same product does in conditions where the design choices that made it work for daily use produce the architectural patterns the walkthroughs name. Both observations can be true at the same time.
