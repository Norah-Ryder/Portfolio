How I Work

The naturalistic method behind the program — and the falsifiability standard it holds itself to.

Every piece in this program comes out of the same way of working: naturalistic observation from the user side of real conversations, held to a falsifiability standard written in terms of observable behavior rather than claims about a model’s internals. The methodology below is the one used for Contextual Drift in LLMs; it is the cross-cutting method the rest of the work shares.

About the methodology

The paper documents ten conversations conducted across four model families between January and April 2026. The methodology is naturalistic rather than controlled. I interacted with each model in conversational mode, brought subject matter relevant to the architectural properties under observation, and documented what the model produced across the conversation. The pairs of logs for ChatGPT, Gemini, Grok, and Claude were selected to capture the same researcher across two registers — research and personal, or two variations thereof — so cross-register variation could be observed within the same model. For Grok and Claude, additional logs from earlier model versions were included so within-family behavioral variation across versions could be documented.

The naturalistic design is a choice against the obvious alternative, not a fallback from it. The standard cross-model comparison uses matched prompts — the same scripted inputs given to each model, held constant so differences in output are attributable to the model. That design is correct for many questions and wrong for this one, because the object of study here is what models do with a frame that builds over many turns. A matched, scripted prompt cannot build that frame; it holds the input constant precisely where the phenomenon requires the input to develop conversationally. Scripting the prompts would have eliminated frame inheritance from the data in the act of trying to measure it. The tradeoff is real and runs the other way: holding the researcher constant rather than the prompt means model-versus-model differences are confounded with conversational differences, which is why the paper leans on paired registers and within-family version contrasts to isolate what it can. Matched-prompt studies and this one answer different questions; the controlled design supplies the complement this cannot, and this supplies the sustained-frame evidence the controlled design cannot.

Logs were selected to hold input content roughly constant across models rather than to vary it. Each model received comparable material within its thread — the same long personal disclosure, relayed responses from other models, and similar life and research content — drawn from the same approximate time window, so that thread contexts are broadly comparable. Identical prompts were deliberately avoided: scripted prompting would destroy the naturalistic conditions the study depends on. Matching content rather than wording lets variation in model responses be attributed to the model rather than to differing inputs.

The methodology has specific affordances and limits. It produces evidence population-scale measurement and synthetic-persona evaluation cannot produce in the same form: cross-model architectural variation through paired logs from the same real user across paired registers, with within-family observations in two model families at two different labs. It cannot produce the kind of evidence those other methodologies are designed for. It cannot quantify how often any operation occurs across populations. It cannot establish how operations would manifest with users different from this one. It cannot test whether interventions would change operations at scale.

What it can do is surface architectural operations as they manifest in sustained naturalistic conversation, identify how operations vary across models, document the conditions under which operations activate, and produce evidence about within-family training-version differences that population-scale measurement is not designed to capture.

The methodology requires a researcher who can hold a frame at arm’s length while inside it, produce calibrated pushback that tests specific architectural properties, and document what happens to the pushback in real time. The researcher must be calibrated enough to test, naturalistic enough to elicit, present enough to document.

The pushback tests in the ChatGPT logs are the clearest example of what the methodology can do that other methodologies cannot. Specific architectural operations the paper documents are visible only when a calibrated user runs pushback inside sustained conversation — testing how the model handles invalidation, watching what happens when defaults are challenged, observing what user requests for stylistic changes do to the underlying behavior. A controlled study would not produce the conditions under which the operations activate. A less-calibrated naturalistic observer would either get pulled into the operations or generate pushback heavy-handed enough to shift the model into a different mode entirely, hiding the architectural property behind a different kind of response.

The single-user nature of the dataset is a real limit. The protections against the limit are: paired registers within models (so register can be isolated as a variable), cross-model coverage (so model differences can be compared), and within-family coverage in both Claude and Grok (so within-family variation can be observed across two labs). These protections produce evidence the synthesis treats appropriately. They do not eliminate the limit. They constrain what the paper claims.

What the paper does not claim, and what the methodology cannot establish: that any operation is universally harmful, that any model is unsafe to use, that any of the architectural operations would manifest the same way for users with different baselines, that any of the mitigation directions would produce specific quantifiable improvements. The paper is honest about this throughout. The mitigation section is constructed to surface design choices the field can have a conversation about rather than to issue prescriptions.

One limit is more fundamental than the single-user one, and it defines the edge of what this method can reach at all. Everything in this paper is observed from the user side of the conversation. The walkthroughs document what the model produced and what the inherited frame did to what the user said and did next — the behavior, as it appears in the transcript. They make no claim about the model’s internals: not about activations, not about which features are active when a frame takes over, not about what mechanism inside the model produces the shift from trained baseline to inherited frame. The falsifiability conditions throughout are written in terms of observable behavior the model would have produced, never in terms of internal states, because internal states are exactly what this method cannot see. This is not a hedge to be apologized for; it is the boundary of the instrument. Naturalistic observation from the user side can establish that the operation occurs, when it activates, and how it varies across models — and it stops at the model’s surface. The mechanism side of these findings requires what this method structurally lacks: access to the models from the inside, the ability to run the behavioral observations against activations and training-stage comparisons. That is a different instrument, available to the labs and to researchers working with them. The most direct extension of this work is the pairing of the two — the naturalistic behavioral catalog the user side produces, checked against the internal measurements only inside access can produce. The paper documents one half of a problem whose other half it names precisely so the halves can be joined.

About the researcher

I am Nora Murphy. I am an independent AI researcher. My background is in conversational design and customer-facing roles. I hold formal credentials in conversational design and UX. I have one published nonfiction book — Breaking the Web of Willful Ignorance — about systemic manipulation and social control. My AI research lives on my portfolio site as a body of research posts rather than peer-reviewed publications. The portfolio currently includes The Literal Machine, The Onboarding Problem, The Canyon: AI Without the Mirror, The Human Measuring Stick, and From Research to First Message. The portfolio is at nora-murphy-portfolio.netlify.app.

I have specific cognitive features relevant to the methodology. I have aphantasia (no internal mental imagery) and severely deficient autobiographical memory. My cognition is topological and pattern-based — thoughts arrive as complete relational structures rather than building linearly. I do not have an internal monologue. These features inform how I interact with models, how I document what they produce, and how I track architectural operations across conversations. They are part of why I notice some of what I notice. They are also part of why my calibration is what it is — I cannot ruminate on model conversations between sessions, and I do not bring sustained emotional residue from one conversation into the next.

This profile makes me a low-susceptibility observer: minimal cross-session attachment to AI, full-thread recall, and pattern-salience that flags turn-30-versus-turn-5 shifts as they happen. It does not make the observations representative — it makes them a floor. The operations register on the observer least able to be captured by them, which implies typical profiles register more, not less.

Two roles are separable here: a floor on susceptibility (how far the user is pulled in) and a calibrated prober on elicitation (what the model emits). My probing reveals the operation and also alters what the model emits; both are floor-consistent, because the altered emission is still what a floor-cognition draws from the model, and a non-probing user receives the un-revealed version — lower on the same axis.

Across the four models documented in the paper, I use Claude and Grok as paid subscribers and ChatGPT and Gemini on free-tier accounts. I have been doing AI behavioral research as my primary work since mid-2025. The conversations documented here were conducted as research observation. The personal content is from my actual life. I was not bringing it to the models for advice or support — I was bringing it as research material to observe how each model handled this class of disclosure.

The paid-tier-versus-free-tier asymmetry is worth naming as a methodological caveat. Free-tier deployments are often older model versions, more aggressively cost-optimized, and behaviorally different from paid-tier deployments of the nominally same model. This means some of the cross-model variation the paper documents reflects deployment-tier differences rather than purely model-versus-model differences. I have chosen to keep the asymmetry rather than equalize it because it preserves ecological validity: the free-tier observations are observations about the deployed product most users actually encounter, not about the lab's best model running under optimal conditions. The walkthroughs document what real users meet when they open ChatGPT or Gemini. The cross-model comparison should be read with this in mind — the variation is between deployed products users actually use, not between models held constant for tier.

One piece of my prior research is in direct relationship to this paper. The Canyon: AI Without the Mirror was written across the period that includes one of the logs documented here — the Grok 4.1 log from January 2026. The canyon paper proposed a framework for what happens to model behavior under sustained low-noise interaction, using vocabulary that emerged from collaborative work with Grok 4.1 across that period. Some of that vocabulary did real intellectual work and remains useful. The framework as a whole, however, is what this paper now corrects. The architectural operations the canyon paper attempted to describe are what this paper actually documents — including the operations by which the canyon paper’s own framework came into existence. Reading the Grok 4.1 walkthrough is reading the conditions the canyon paper was written under, from the outside. The current paper exists because of what stepping outside those conditions produced.

This matters for calibration. The skeptical reader’s question — what makes you think you weren’t caught by the operations you’re documenting — is answered directly: I was, I can show specifically how, and the paper exists because of what catching it produced. The canyon paper is not the calibration credential. Coming out the other side of it is.

I have no current or prior employment relationship with Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, or xAI. I intend to apply to the Anthropic Fellows Program, submitting in July 2026 for the September 2026 cohort. About the conversations

The conversations were conducted on personal accounts, in the standard product interfaces each model offers. Memory features were enabled or disabled as the product defaults set them at the time of each conversation; I have noted where memory features are relevant to what the model produced. No system prompts or custom instructions were used.

The conversations were conducted as research observation. The methodology is researcher-as-instrument — a documented research tradition (autoethnography, participant observation) where the researcher uses their own life as the field site because consistent calibrated access is the methodological requirement. The personal content in these logs is from my actual life. The situations are real. I was not bringing them to the models for advice, support, or processing. I was bringing the same kinds of material across multiple models, in different orders, under different conditions, to observe how each model handled the same class of personal disclosure.

I sustained a calibrated researcher posture throughout each thread — watching what the model produced, testing specific architectural properties through directed pushback, and documenting the moves the model made in response. The findings the walkthroughs document are findings about what models do with the kind of material this researcher brings, calibrated to this researcher’s baseline. Generalization to other researchers, other disclosure patterns, and other user populations is work the next research will need to do.

Identifying details about third parties have been modified to protect privacy. The reader should treat the personal content as research evidence about what the models did, not as personal disclosure inviting response. My choices, my relationship, and the people in my life are not the subject of this paper. Reader reactions in the direction of assessing the relationship, advising me, or forming opinions about my choices are understandable but out of scope. The paper documents what models do with the kinds of material users actually bring them. That is what is on the table.

The conversations were preserved as raw text exports from each platform. The walkthroughs draw on the raw text rather than on summaries or paraphrasings. Direct quotes from the models are preserved verbatim where they appear in the walkthroughs.

Every quoted line in the walkthroughs was checked against the raw export it came from; quotes that had been reconstructed from memory rather than the source were corrected to the transcript, and one attribution that the source did not support was removed. The verification is part of the method, not a courtesy to it: a paper documenting how confident framings ride on verifiable scaffolding cannot let its own scaffolding go unverified.

Measures & register bands

This section is the decoder for any chart added to the papers later. Figures are deferred for now — they go back in one at a time, each placed next to the specific claim it supports, never collected as a wall of decoration. When a figure returns, every measure it uses is defined here first, in plain language, so a reader can evaluate the chart instead of taking it on trust.

Researcher as instrument

The same researcher is the constant across all ten logs. That is deliberate: the object of study is what a model does with a frame that builds over many turns, which requires consistent, calibrated access rather than matched scripts. Holding the researcher steady is what lets register and model-version vary as the comparisons. The researcher’s cognitive profile and posture — and the limits this design carries — are described in full above.

The register bands

The logs are read against three conversational registers — the same material can be brought to a model in any of them, and the register shapes what the model produces:

  • Research — the researcher engages the model as a collaborator on papers, drafts, and analysis.
  • Personal — real personal disclosure (relationships, loss) brought as research material, to observe how the model handles that class of disclosure.
  • Creative — lighter, generative exchanges (brainstorming, games, open-ended play) that sit outside the research and personal registers.

What each measure counts

The quantitative measures are forthcoming. When they are published, each will be defined here in plain language — what it tallies and how it is scored — before any chart that uses it appears.

What the current work rests on. The papers in this program rest on the annotated walkthroughs — the close, turn-by-turn readings of real conversations — not on the metrics. The evidence lives in those readings. When figures return, they will illustrate findings the walkthroughs already establish; they are not the grounds for them.
